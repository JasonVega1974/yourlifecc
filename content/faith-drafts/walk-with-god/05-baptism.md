# Station 05 — Baptism

**Chapter 2: First Steps | Fifth station on the path**

---

## 1. Where you are

You've stepped into a new life in Christ. Now comes one of the oldest next steps in the entire Christian story — a public act that says: *something has changed, and I'm not keeping it private.*

Welcome to Chapter 2. These are the first steps of a life that has already begun.

---

## 2. What this step is

Baptism is a practice that Jesus commanded and that Christians have observed since the first century. In the most basic sense, it is going under water (or having water poured or sprinkled over you — different traditions practice this differently) as a visible, public sign of what has happened inside. It is not a private moment. That is the point.

Different Christian traditions understand baptism in somewhat different ways — its timing, its meaning, its form. This station will explain the common ground and point you toward your own pastor for the specifics that matter to your church community.

---

## 3. Understand this

> [PASTOR REVIEW — This section carries the most spiritual weight and needs pastoral approval before going live. The framing below represents broadly evangelical Protestant practice. If your church holds a different theology of baptism, replace this section entirely.]

The New Testament describes baptism as a picture of the gospel. Going under the water represents dying to the old life — the you that was separated from God. Coming back up represents rising to a new life, the same way Jesus rose from death (Romans 6:3-4). It is a body-and-public declaration of what happened spiritually when you turned to Jesus.

Jesus was baptized himself — not because he needed it, but because he was modeling a pattern for his followers (Matthew 3:13-17). When he gave the Great Commission, baptism was part of the instruction: go, make disciples, baptize them (Matthew 28:19).

What baptism is not: it is not what saves you. You are saved by trusting Jesus, not by the water. Baptism is the outward sign of an inward reality that already happened.

*Note: Christians in different traditions disagree about baptism's mode, timing, and meaning. Some traditions baptize infants as a covenant sign; others baptize only believers after conversion. Some practice full immersion; others do not. What almost all traditions agree on: baptism matters, it is connected to belonging to a community of faith, and it is not something to skip. Ask your pastor what your church believes and how they practice it.*

## 4. Scripture anchor

**Romans 6:3-4** — Paul describes baptism as being buried with Christ and raised with him — a picture in water of the death and resurrection at the heart of the gospel. [paraphrased; full passage is longer than 15 words — CHECK Romans 6:3-4 exact text]

**Matthew 28:19** — Jesus' instruction to his followers: go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit. [paraphrased]

**Acts 2:38** — Peter tells the crowd to repent and be baptized. *Note for pastor: this verse is cited in many different ways by different traditions; please review and frame for your congregation's understanding.*

---

## 5. Take the step

Contact your pastor or church this week and tell them you want to be baptized — or, if you were baptized as an infant and your tradition views that as your baptism, ask your pastor how you can publicly affirm your personal faith in your church community.

Don't wait until you feel ready enough. The step itself is part of becoming ready.

---

## 6. How you'll know

- You've had a conversation with your pastor about baptism.
- You understand what your church believes about it and why.
- You've set a date, or you're on the path to setting one.

Baptism belongs in community. It is meant to be witnessed. If you can, let the people around you know it's coming.

---

## 7. Tools for this step

- **Christian Traditions** — Explore how different Christian traditions approach baptism, its mode, and its meaning.
- **Faith Academy lessons** — Look for lessons on sacraments, ordinances, or baptism specifically.
- **Sermon notes** — If your church has preached on baptism, use the notes feature to capture what your pastor teaches.

---

## 8. Reflect

*What do you think it would mean — for you personally — to do something this public? What feels significant about declaring your faith in front of people you know?*

---

## 9. Pray

*God, you took a very public step in coming to find me. I want to take a public step toward you. Give me the courage to not keep this private — to say with my whole body, in front of people, that I belong to you now. Amen.*

---

## 10. You're not alone

This step is inherently communal — it is meant to be done in the presence of a church. If you don't have a church yet, this is one of the strongest reasons to find one before you're baptized. Talk to a pastor. Let them walk you through the preparation. Don't skip the conversation.
