# Station 07 — Rooted in the Word

**Chapter 2: First Steps | Seventh station on the path**

---

## 1. Where you are

You're learning to pray. Now the other half of the relationship opens up: listening. And the primary way God has spoken — consistently, across thousands of years — is through the words that make up the Bible.

This station is for anyone who has never opened it, or opened it and gotten confused, or opened it and given up.

---

## 2. What this step is

Reading Scripture is not a religious chore. It's how you get to know someone. The Bible is not primarily a rulebook or a history textbook (though it contains both). It is the story of God pursuing human beings — and learning to read it is learning to know the God you've just said yes to.

This station will not make you a Bible scholar. It will help you start.

---

## 3. Understand this

> [PASTOR REVIEW — This section carries the most spiritual weight and needs pastoral approval before going live.]

The Bible says something remarkable about itself: that it is "breathed out" by God — that the human authors wrote under a kind of divine direction that makes Scripture more than just human wisdom (2 Timothy 3:16-17). That's a big claim, and it's worth thinking about. But for a new reader, the more practical truth is this: Scripture is *alive* in a way that other books are not. The same passage will mean something different to you at 20 and at 50, in a season of peace and in a season of crisis. People have read these words for thousands of years and they keep finding something new (Hebrews 4:12).

The Bible is not designed to be read front to back like a novel. For new readers, a better starting point is:

- **The Gospel of John** — The most direct introduction to who Jesus is and what he claimed.
- **Psalms** — The emotional range of a human life, brought to God in prayer.
- **Romans** — If you want to understand the logic of the gospel in depth.

Reading plans (available in the app) can take you through sections day by day so you're never staring at an open page wondering where to start.

What to do when you don't understand something: write the question down, don't panic, and keep reading. Much of Scripture explains itself if you give it time. For the questions that don't resolve, that's what pastors, commentaries, and Bible study groups are for.

## 4. Scripture anchor

**Psalm 119:105** — "Your word is a lamp for my feet, a light on my path." — not a floodlight; enough light for the next step.

**2 Timothy 3:16-17** — Paul says all Scripture is God-breathed and useful — for teaching, correcting, training in what is right — so that a person following God is equipped for every good thing ahead. [paraphrased; full passage is longer than 15 words]

**Hebrews 4:12** — "For the word of God is alive and active." — not a static document; something that engages you when you engage it. [first 9 words are a direct quote; the rest is paraphrase — CHECK VERSE]

---

## 5. Take the step

Start a reading plan today — even the shortest one available. Three days, five minutes a day. You don't have to read a lot. You have to start. Open the Bible reader and read one chapter of John (start with John 1). Write down one thing that surprised you.

---

## 6. How you'll know

- You've read the Bible at least once this week — not as a religious obligation, but as a conversation.
- You've found at least one verse or passage that felt like it was written for where you are right now.
- You want to know what comes next in the story.

---

## 7. Tools for this step

- **Bible reader** — The full text of Scripture, organized and searchable.
- **Reading plans** — Structured daily plans that give you a clear starting point and a daily portion.
- **Scripture memory** — Once you find a verse that lands, this is how you carry it with you. Memory is one of the oldest spiritual disciplines.
- **Story Mode** — A narrative overview of the whole Bible that helps you understand the big picture before you dive into chapters.
- **Timeline** — Puts the events of Scripture into historical context.

---

## 8. Reflect

*If God wanted to tell you something right now — through a word, a verse, a passage — what do you think he might say? What would you most need to hear?*

---

## 9. Pray

*God, I want to hear you — not just talk to you. I'm going to open this book and try. I know I won't understand everything. Help me find what you want me to find. And when I get confused, remind me that you're patient with people who are still learning the language. Amen.*

---

## 10. You're not alone

The Bible was not designed to be read alone. It has been read, debated, and wrestled with in community for thousands of years. Consider joining a Bible study group at your church — even a small one. Reading together changes what you see.

Your pastor is also a resource. Most pastors love the question "I read this and didn't understand it — can you help me?" That's one of the best conversations you can have with a pastor.
