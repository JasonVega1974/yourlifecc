# Station 13 — Becoming Like Christ

**Chapter — The Horizon | The thirteenth and final station**

---

## 1. Where you are

You've walked a long way. Curiosity led to understanding. Understanding led to surrender. Surrender led to roots — in prayer, in Scripture, in community. The roots led to fruit. The fruit is beginning to spill over into other people's lives.

And still, you are not done. You will not be done. That is not a failure — it is the design.

This station marks the horizon: the direction the whole path has been pointing, the person you are slowly becoming, the work that will not be finished until the very end.

---

## 2. What this step is

Becoming like Christ is the theological word theologians call "sanctification" — the ongoing, lifelong process of being made holy, being formed into the image of Jesus, being shaped from the inside out into someone who naturally loves what Jesus loves and lives how Jesus lived.

It is not a station you complete. It is a direction you face. The walk itself is the destination — and there is always more horizon ahead.

---

## 3. Understand this

> [PASTOR REVIEW — This section carries the most spiritual weight and needs pastoral approval before going live.]

Paul writes to the Philippians with a confidence that is not confidence in himself: he is certain that the One who started a good work in them will keep going, all the way to completion (Philippians 1:6). That's the architecture of sanctification — God begins it and God finishes it, and you cooperate with a process that is ultimately his work, not yours.

Romans 8:29 says that God's design for his children is to be conformed to the image of his Son — that they would look like Jesus in increasing measure. And 2 Corinthians 3:18 says this is not a one-time event: it is a transformation with ever-increasing glory, coming from the Lord himself.

Paul's own testimony — and he is writing after years and years of following Jesus — is striking. "Not that I have already obtained all this," he says, "or have already arrived at my goal, but I press on" (Philippians 3:12). [CHECK — VERIFY this is Philippians 3:12 exact quote NIV/ESV; sense is correct] Paul, who wrote half the New Testament, considered himself still becoming.

That should be freeing. The pressure to have arrived, to be spiritually mature enough, to have it together — it is not a pressure Jesus put on you. What he put on you is an invitation: keep walking. Keep pressing. Keep facing the horizon.

The whole path you've walked — the curiosity, the gospel, the surrender, the roots, the community, the Spirit, the discipline, the service, the disciple-making — it is all one long act of becoming. None of it is wasted. None of it is finished. All of it is moving you somewhere.

## 5. Take the step

Write down one way you are different from who you were when you first began this walk — or this year, or five years ago. One thing that is genuinely more Christlike in you than it used to be. Acknowledge it. Thank God for it. Then write one thing you want to look like in five years, and bring it to him in prayer.

---

## 6. How you'll know

There is no finish line here. But these are signs the transformation is real:

- You love people you wouldn't have been able to love before.
- You are less afraid of being honest about your own failures — because you know they don't define you.
- You find yourself wanting what Jesus wants, caring about what Jesus cares about, more than you used to.
- When you read about Jesus in Scripture, you recognize someone you are becoming, not just someone you are admiring from a distance.

These will all be true in partial, imperfect measure. That's not a problem. It's the walk.

---

## 7. Tools for this step

- **Prayer journal** — Looking back at where you've prayed from in past seasons shows you the transformation you might not see day to day.
- **Scripture memory** — Carrying the words of Scripture in your mind is one of the most direct ways the Spirit transforms how you think.
- **Timeline** — Putting your own story inside the much larger story of God at work through history can give you perspective on where you are on the path.
- **Faith Academy lessons** — The deeper lessons on sanctification, the character of Christ, and spiritual formation give language to what is happening in you.

---

## 8. Reflect

*Who is the person you are becoming? If you could write a letter from your future self — five years further along the path — what would it say to who you are right now? What is the most important thing still ahead?*

---

## 9. Pray

*God, I'm not done. I know I'm not done. And I'm okay with that — because you promised that you are. Keep working in me in the places I can't see. Thank you for every step of the path — the beginning, the stumbles, the seasons when I couldn't feel you and kept walking anyway. I'm still here. I'm still yours. Don't stop. Amen.*

---

## 10. You're not alone

The people who will walk with you over the coming decades are the most important people in your spiritual formation. Invest in those relationships. Be honest with a mentor about where you're still stuck. Let your church community see the real version of the walk, not just the presentable version.

And when you are further along the path — walk back. The person behind you needs what you've been given.

---

## 11. Next step

There is no next station — this is the horizon.

But every station on this path is still open. Return to any of them as often as you need. The person who has walked the whole path is still a person who prays, reads Scripture, belongs to a community, serves others, and makes disciples. The stations are not stages you complete; they are practices you deepen.

The walk continues. Keep going.
