# Station 10 — Discipleship

**Chapter 3: Growing Deep | Tenth station on the path**

---

## 1. Where you are

You've been walking with God long enough to know that the easy early days are giving way to something harder and better: the long, steady work of becoming someone different. This station is about the tools and habits that shape that becoming.

---

## 2. What this step is

A disciple is a learner — someone who has chosen a teacher and is intentionally becoming like them. Christian discipleship means choosing Jesus as that teacher and structuring your life so that the shaping can actually happen.

This is not a weekend sprint. It is a long walk. The good news is that the person doing the shaping is patient, thorough, and better at this than you are.

---

## 3. Understand this

> [PASTOR REVIEW — This section carries the most spiritual weight and needs pastoral approval before going live.]

Jesus said that anyone who wants to follow him must do three things: deny themselves, take up their cross daily, and follow him (Luke 9:23). That's not soft language. Discipleship has a cost — the cost of redirecting your life around someone other than yourself.

But it is also a path toward the only kind of life that holds up. Paul says the things of Christ should "dwell richly" among believers — teaching, encouraging, worshipping together, doing everything in Jesus' name (Colossians 3:16-17). That's a life that has intentional shape to it.

The practices that form disciples have been tested across centuries. They are often called "spiritual disciplines." Here are the ones most commonly named:

- **Scripture reading and meditation** — not just reading, but sitting with a passage until something opens.
- **Prayer** — extended, unhurried time with God, not just quick requests.
- **Fasting** — voluntarily going without something (usually food, sometimes other things) to sharpen focus on God.
- **Solitude and silence** — withdrawing from noise and stimulation to listen.
- **Generosity** — giving money, time, and resources as a practice, not an occasional impulse.
- **Service** — acting for others' benefit without personal gain.
- **Accountability** — being in a relationship with someone who can ask you honest questions and hear honest answers.
- **Worship** — individual and corporate, as a regular shaping practice, not just an emotion.

You don't need to take all of these on at once. Start with one or two. The point is that you are deliberately making space for God to work on you.

Discipleship also involves being discipled by someone — a mentor, a pastor, a small-group leader — and eventually discipling someone else. That cycle is how the Christian faith has moved through generations (2 Timothy 2:2).

## 5. Take the step

Pick one spiritual discipline you haven't tried yet — or one you've tried and stopped. Practice it once this week. Not every day, not perfectly, not forever. Once. Then decide if it's worth adding to your regular rhythm.

---

## 6. How you'll know

- You have at least one spiritual practice that is now part of your regular routine — not because you "should," but because it is genuinely forming you.
- You have at least one person who knows the real state of your spiritual life and is walking with you.
- You find yourself being more patient, more honest, more present — not all the time, but more often than a year ago.

---

## 7. Tools for this step

- **Faith Academy lessons** — Structured teaching that covers the core topics a disciple needs to understand.
- **Reading plans** — Extended reading plans (30-day, 90-day) that build the Scripture-reading muscle.
- **Scripture memory** — Possibly the most underrated discipline. Memory transforms what you carry into everything.
- **Sermon notes** — Engaging with your pastor's teaching in a disciplined, written way deepens what you receive.
- **Prayer journal** — Writing your prayers is itself a spiritual discipline that creates a record of growth.

---

## 8. Reflect

*What is one area of your life where you most want to look different in five years? What practice or relationship would actually help you get there?*

---

## 9. Pray

*God, I want to be formed, not just informed. Take me past the things I know in my head and into the things that are actually changing who I am. Show me the next practice, the next discipline, the next relationship that needs to be in my life. I'm not in a hurry — but I don't want to stay where I am. Amen.*

---

## 10. You're not alone

Discipleship is inherently relational. Find a mentor — someone who is further along the path than you are — and ask them to meet with you regularly. This is one of the most countercultural and most powerful things you can do. Most people who are further along the path would be honored to be asked. Ask your pastor how your church structures discipleship relationships; many churches have a formal process.
