## The 30 questions

### who-made-god
**Category:** Origins / existence of God
**Question:** If God created everything, who created God?
**Answer:** This is one of the oldest questions people have asked, and it deserves a real answer, not a shrug. Everything we experience needs a cause because it exists inside time — it started, so something started it. The Christian claim is that God isn't another thing inside that chain needing a cause; He's the eternal, uncaused source everything else came from, existing outside time altogether. That's not a dodge — nearly every serious thinker on this question, believer or not, ends up admitting something has to be eternal and uncaused. The real disagreement is over what that something is: an eternal personal God, or an eternal impersonal universe. Keep pushing on this one; it's a fair question, and it's supposed to make you think.
**Scripture:** Genesis 1:1 — "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth."
**Explore further:** Ask the Bible; Christian Life Guide
**Pastor review needed:** no

### why-believe-god-exists
**Category:** Origins / existence of God
**Question:** Why should I even believe there's a God? I've never seen Him.
**Answer:** Fair question — you shouldn't believe things just because you're told to. Christians point to a few different threads that, taken together, make belief reasonable: the universe having a beginning rather than existing forever, the fine-tuned conditions that make life possible at all, the widespread human sense that right and wrong are real and not just opinion, and — for many people — a personal sense of being known that showed up over time, not from one dramatic moment. None of these alone is a slam-dunk proof; together, a lot of thoughtful people find them convincing. This isn't a "just have faith and stop asking" answer — the evidence is meant to be examined, not avoided.
**Scripture:** Psalm 19:1 — "The heavens declare the glory of God." [CHECK — verify exact translation wording]

### faith-without-evidence
**Category:** Origins / existence of God
**Question:** Isn't faith just believing something without any evidence — like wishful thinking?
**Answer:** That's actually not what the Bible means by faith, even though the word gets used that way in everyday conversation. Biblical faith is closer to trust — the kind you place in a person or a claim because of what you've seen and heard, not in spite of it. You already do this constantly: you trust a chair will hold you, a friend will keep their word, a plane will stay in the air — all faith built on evidence, not blind hope. Christian faith works the same way — it's trust placed in a God who has left real reasons to believe He's there and trustworthy.
**Scripture:** Hebrews 11:1 — "faith is confidence in what we hope for, assurance about what we do not see." [CHECK — verify exact translation wording]
**Explore further:** Proof & Prophecy (full collection); Christian Life Guide
**Pastor review needed:** no

### why-does-god-want-worship
**Category:** Origins / existence of God
**Question:** Why does God even want us to worship Him? Isn't that kind of full of Himself for someone who's supposed to be loving?
**Answer:** It sounds that way if you picture worship like a person fishing for compliments. But worship in the Bible isn't God topping off His ego — it's us telling the truth about what's actually going on: He made everything, He sustains everything, He's the most valuable thing there is. A plant doesn't need light because the sun is needy; it needs light because that's how life works. Worship is less about God needing applause and more about us being rightly oriented toward reality — and, honestly, people tend to become like whatever they worship, so it matters a lot what (or who) you aim that at.
**Scripture:** Psalm 100:3 — a call to "know that the LORD is God" and that He made us. [CHECK — verify exact translation wording; paraphrased, verse is longer than 15 words]

### bad-things-good-people
**Category:** Problem of evil / suffering
**Question:** If God is good, why do bad things keep happening to good people?
**Answer:** This is genuinely one of the hardest questions in the whole faith, and it deserves more than a tidy answer. Part of the Christian explanation is that the world is broken — not the way God originally made it, but the way it's become through a long history of human rebellion and its ripple effects (sickness, decay, cruelty, disaster). That doesn't make the pain make sense on the day it happens to you. What the Bible does promise is that God is not distant from it — He entered it Himself in Jesus, and He's at work even inside suffering, not just around it, moving things toward real good even when we can't see how yet.
**Scripture:** Romans 8:28 — the promise that "in all things God works for the good of those who love him." [CHECK — verify exact translation wording, ESV/NIV phrase this differently]

**Pastor review needed:** no

### why-doesnt-god-end-evil-now
**Category:** Problem of evil / suffering
**Question:** If God has the power to get rid of evil, why doesn't He just do it right now?
**Answer:** Here's the uncomfortable part of the answer: ending evil completely, right now, would mean ending it everywhere it lives — including inside every one of us. God's patience isn't inaction; it's giving people space to turn toward Him before the final reckoning comes (which the Bible says is real, and coming). A day is promised when everything genuinely gets set right — no more death, grief, or pain — it's just not deferred forever, and it's not happening on our timeline.
**Scripture:** Revelation 21:4 — "There will be no more death or mourning or crying or pain." [CHECK — verify exact translation wording]

### why-hasnt-god-destroyed-the-devil
**Category:** Problem of evil / suffering
**Question:** If God is more powerful than the devil, why hasn't He just destroyed him already?
**Answer:** Christians believe this already happened, in the most important sense — Jesus's death and resurrection broke Satan's ultimate power, even though the full, final removal is still ahead. Think of it less like an ongoing fight God might lose and more like a defeated enemy still causing damage on the way out the door. It can feel confusing living in that "already, but not yet" stretch of time — real damage still happening from a power that's already been beaten — but that tension is honestly part of the Christian story, not a loophole in it.
**Scripture:** 1 John 3:8 — "the reason the Son of God appeared was to destroy the devil's work."

It also matters that God isn't distant from this grief — Jesus wept at a friend's grave, even knowing He was about to raise him. That doesn't make the loss hurt less today, and the ultimate hope of a future where death is undone doesn't erase present grief either — both are true at once. ### why-do-natural-disasters-happen
**Category:** Problem of evil / suffering
**Question:** If God is in control of everything, why do earthquakes and hurricanes and floods happen?
**Answer:** The Bible actually describes the physical world itself as being caught up in the same brokenness people are — not functioning exactly the way it was designed to, "groaning" under the weight of it, the way our bodies and relationships are too. That's different from saying every disaster is a specific, targeted punishment for specific sin — the Bible actually pushes back hard on that idea (ask about Job's friends sometime). Natural disasters are part of living in a world that isn't yet fully what it's meant to become — genuinely painful, and genuinely temporary.
**Scripture:** Romans 8:22 — "the whole creation has been groaning as in the pains of childbirth." [CHECK — verify exact translation wording]

### loving-god-and-hell
**Category:** Hell / judgment
**Question:** How can a loving God send people to hell? That doesn't sound loving at all.
**Answer:** This is a fair objection and shouldn't be waved away. The Christian answer isn't that God enjoys condemning people — quite the opposite: He doesn't want anyone to end up separated from Him, and He went to the length of the cross specifically to prevent it. Hell, in most Christian understanding, is less a place God throws people into and more the natural, final result of a life spent persistently, freely refusing the only source of real good — love that never allows for a genuine "no" isn't love, it's control. Christians disagree on some of the specifics of what hell actually is (more on that in the next question) — but everyone agrees this is one of the heaviest truths in the whole Bible and shouldn't be talked about casually.
**Scripture:** John 3:17 — "God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world." [CHECK — verify exact translation wording]

### people-who-never-heard
**Category:** Hell / judgment
**Question:** What happens to someone who lived and died without ever hearing about Jesus — is that fair?
**Answer:** This is one of the questions where thoughtful, Bible-believing Christians genuinely land in different places, so here's the honest version rather than a confident one. Every Christian tradition agrees that God is perfectly just, knows every person's heart better than we ever could, and judges people fairly based on what they actually had access to and did with it. Some traditions hold that explicit faith in Christ during this life is necessary regardless of access; others believe God's mercy and justice extend further than we can map for those who genuinely never had the chance to hear. What everyone agrees on: this isn't ours to decide from the outside, and it's exactly the kind of question that should make us want more people to hear the good news, not fewer.
**Scripture:** Genesis 18:25 — "will not the Judge of all the earth do right?"

**Pastor review needed:** yes — see pastor decision list #2.

### is-hell-literal-fire-forever
**Category:** Hell / judgment
**Question:** Is hell an actual place with fire that never ends, or is that more symbolic?
**Answer:** Christians who take the Bible seriously actually disagree here, so it's worth being honest about that instead of picking a side quietly. Some read the fire-and-darkness language literally; others read it as vivid, true imagery for a real but different kind of loss and separation; some believe final judgment ends in destruction rather than unending conscious suffering. What every serious reading shares is this: hell is described as a real, devastating, permanent separation from God — not a minor inconvenience, and not nothing. It's worth taking the weight of that seriously even before settling the mechanics.

### bible-changed-mistranslated
**Category:** Bible reliability
**Question:** Hasn't the Bible been translated and retranslated so many times that we can't trust what it actually says anymore?
**Answer:** This is a really common assumption, but it's based on a myth — that the Bible got passed down like a game of telephone, English to English, generation after generation, drifting further from the original each time. That's not how it works. We have thousands of manuscript copies of the Bible in the original Hebrew and Greek, some dating within decades or a couple centuries of the originals — closer to the source than almost any other ancient document we trust as history. Every modern translation goes back to those early manuscripts directly, not to an earlier English version. Scholars can and do cross-check them against each other, and the level of agreement across thousands of copies is remarkable.
**Scripture:** Isaiah 40:8 — "the word of our God endures forever."
**Explore further:** Proof & Prophecy — Manuscripts category (Dead Sea Scrolls, Codex Sinaiticus); Ask the Bible
**Pastor review needed:** no

### how-do-we-know-bible-true
**Category:** Bible reliability
**Question:** How do we actually know the Bible is true, and not just an old book of stories?
**Answer:** This is worth a deeper dive than one answer can give, so the short version: there are a few independent lines of evidence that reinforce each other — manuscript reliability (we can check what the earliest copies actually said), archaeological and historical confirmation (places, people, and events the Bible mentions keep getting verified by outside sources), and fulfilled prophecy (specific predictions written well before the events they describe). On top of that, the Bible holds together as one consistent story despite being written by 40-plus authors across roughly 1,500 years — that kind of coherence is unusual for a "just stories" collection.
**Scripture:** 2 Timothy 3:16 — "All Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for teaching."
**Explore further:** Proof & Prophecy (full 100-entry collection); Ask the Bible
**Pastor review needed:** no

### bible-written-by-men-made-up
**Category:** Bible reliability
**Question:** The Bible was written by regular people — how do we know they didn't just make it up?
**Answer:** "Written by people" and "made up by people" aren't the same thing — most books of the Bible explicitly claim to be based on eyewitness accounts or close, careful investigation of eyewitnesses, not campfire legend. And here's a detail worth sitting with: many of the people who wrote or carried this message suffered real loss, and some were executed, for refusing to take it back — that's not the usual profile of people protecting a story they know is fake. None of that proves every detail by itself, but it's a strong reason to take the claim seriously rather than dismiss it as ancient fan fiction.
**Scripture:** 2 Peter 1:16 — "we did not follow cleverly devised stories."
**Explore further:** Proof & Prophecy — Resurrection and Historical Witnesses categories; Ask the Bible
**Pastor review needed:** no

### lose-salvation-after-messing-up
**Category:** Salvation / how it works
**Question:** If I become a Christian and then mess up badly, do I lose my salvation?
**Answer:** This fear is really common in new believers, and Christian traditions genuinely differ on the technical answer, so here's the shared ground first: salvation was never based on your performance to begin with — it's described as a gift, not a paycheck you earn — which means "messing up" doesn't unearn something you never earned in the first place. There's a real difference between a single failure or a relapse you bring back to God in honesty, and a permanent, unrepentant walking-away — different traditions describe that difference differently (some call it "losing salvation," others describe it as evidence it was never really rooted). Whichever camp your church lands in, the invitation is always the same: come back, don't hide.
**Scripture:** Ephesians 2:8 — "it is by grace you have been saved, through faith."

### deathbed-repentance-fairness
**Category:** Salvation / how it works
**Question:** Isn't it unfair that someone who did terrible things their whole life can repent on their deathbed and be forgiven, but a genuinely good person who never believed isn't saved?
**Answer:** This one stings, especially if you actually know someone like "the good person" in that scenario. Here's the reframe: salvation was never about clearing a goodness bar — none of us actually clear it, not even close, so the "good person" isn't missing salvation by a hair, and the deathbed convert isn't sneaking through on a technicality. It's the exact same offer, on the exact same terms, extended to both people the entire time — one of them just waited until the very last possible moment to receive it, at real risk of never getting the chance. It's less "unfair generosity" and more "the same open door the whole way through." The emotional sting of this question is worth sitting with, not just the logic of it.
**Scripture:** Romans 3:23 — "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."
**Explore further:** My Walk — Station 02: Understand the Gospel; Ask the Bible
**Pastor review needed:** yes — see pastor decision list #5.

### is-it-ok-to-doubt
**Category:** Doubt / faith itself
**Question:** Is it actually okay to have doubts about my faith, or does that mean something's wrong with me?
**Answer:** Yes, it's okay — genuinely. Doubt is not the opposite of faith; indifference is. Some of the most respected people in the Bible wrestled with real doubt out loud: a disciple who needed to physically touch Jesus's wounds before he'd believe, a desperate father who told Jesus straight up that his faith and his doubt were both true at the same time. Bringing doubts honestly to God, and to people you trust, tends to grow faith over time — it's hiding doubts behind a performance of certainty that actually stalls people out.
**Scripture:** Mark 9:24 — "I do believe; help me overcome my unbelief!"

**Pastor review needed:** no

### pray-and-nothing-happens
**Category:** Doubt / faith itself
**Question:** What if I pray and nothing happens? Am I doing something wrong?
**Answer:** Prayer isn't a vending machine, and "nothing happened" isn't always the accurate read on what's going on. Sometimes the answer is "not yet," sometimes it's "no," and sometimes it's "yes, but differently than you pictured it" — and sometimes we genuinely miss the answer because we were watching for a specific shape it never took. The Bible actually encourages persistence in prayer rather than a one-and-done attempt, and honesty in what you're actually asking for matters more than getting the "technique" right.
**Scripture:** James 5:16 — "The prayer of a righteous person is powerful and effective."

### how-do-i-know-god-is-listening

**Question:** How do I actually know God is listening — or even real — since I can't see or hear Him?
**Answer:** That's a legitimate thing to wrestle with, and most real relationships start with some kind of evidence before full trust develops anyway. Christians point to a mix of things — creation itself, the Bible's track record, changed lives they've witnessed, and personal experience that builds over time — as reasons that add up, rather than one dramatic lightning-bolt proof. It's also normal for the sense of "knowing" to grow slowly through actually practicing it — praying honestly, paying attention, waiting — rather than arriving all at once, fully formed.

Dry seasons — stretches where prayer and worship feel flat or distant — are common, and well-documented across the history of people who followed God seriously for their whole lives. Faith is trust that keeps showing up regardless of the emotional weather that day; the feelings often do return, sometimes in a different form than before.

### evolution-disprove-bible
**Category:** Science & faith
**Question:** Doesn't evolution basically disprove the Bible and the whole creation story?
**Answer:** Thoughtful, Bible-believing Christians genuinely land in different places on this one, so it's worth naming the range honestly. Some hold a literal, relatively recent six-day creation. Others read the Genesis "days" as a literary framework and are comfortable with an old earth and some form of guided evolutionary process, believing God directed it rather than chance. Both camps affirm the same core claim Genesis is actually making — that God, not chance, is the reason anything exists at all, and that people specifically are made in His image, not just another animal in the lineup. The "which mechanism" debate matters a lot less than getting swept into thinking it settles the "who" question — it doesn't.
**Scripture:** Genesis 1:1 — "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth."
**Explore further:** Proof & Prophecy — Scientific Accuracy category; Ask the Bible
**Pastor review needed:** yes — see pastor decision list #7.

### how-old-is-earth
**Category:** Science & faith
**Question:** How old is the earth, really? Doesn't the Bible say it's only about 6,000 years old?
**Answer:** The 6,000-year figure comes from adding up the genealogies listed in the Bible start to finish — a method some Christians still hold to today. Others read those same genealogies as having intentional gaps (a known pattern in ancient Hebrew genealogies, which often skip generations to make a point) and are comfortable with the earth being billions of years old, as mainstream science holds, seeing Genesis's core claims — God, purposeful creation, humans made in His image — as compatible with either timeframe. This is a live, respectful debate inside the church, not a settled doctrine everyone agrees on.
**Scripture:** 2 Peter 3:8 — "with the Lord a day is like a thousand years."
**Explore further:** Proof & Prophecy; Ask the Bible
**Pastor review needed:** yes — see pastor decision list #8.

### other-religions-are-they-wrong
**Category:** Other religions
**Question:** What about people from other religions — are they just wrong?
**Answer:** This deserves a careful answer, not a dismissive one. Christianity's claim isn't that people of other faiths are foolish or that their traditions are worthless — many contain real moral wisdom, genuine sincerity, and devoted lives. The specific claim is narrower: that Jesus uniquely resolves the problem every religion is, in its own way, trying to solve — the separation between people and God — in a way no other path does. Holding that claim doesn't require looking down on someone who believes differently; it actually requires the opposite — real relationship and real respect, not a debate stance.
**Scripture:** John 14:6 — "I am the way and the truth and the life."
**Explore further:** My Walk — Station 12: Making Disciples; Ask the Bible
**Pastor review needed:** yes — see pastor decision list #9.

### arrogant-to-say-christianity-only-true
**Category:** Other religions
**Question:** Isn't it arrogant to say Christianity is the only true religion?
**Answer:** Here's something worth noticing: even "all religions are equally true" or "no religion is really true" is itself a confident claim about reality that excludes other views — exclusivity isn't unique to Christianity, it's built into having any real conviction about truth at all. What actually matters more than whether a claim is exclusive is whether it's true, and how it's held — with humility and love toward people who see it differently, or with superiority and contempt. Christians are called to hold this specific conviction while genuinely respecting and befriending people who disagree, not treating disagreement as a threat.
**Scripture:** 1 Peter 3:15 — "always be prepared to give an answer... but do this with gentleness and respect." [CHECK — verify exact translation wording; paraphrased/trimmed]

### unanswered-prayers
**Category:** Prayer / God's will
**Question:** Why do some prayers just go unanswered, even really important ones?
**Answer:** It helps to separate "unanswered" from "answered no" or "answered wait" or "answered differently than I asked" — but it's also honest to say that some prayers, especially about real suffering, genuinely don't get the answer we were hoping for, and that's a real and painful part of following God, not a sign you failed at praying correctly. Even Jesus prayed for a way out of what was coming and didn't get it — and prayed through that disappointment anyway, choosing trust over an answer He didn't receive. That's the model, not a magic formula that guarantees the outcome you want.

### how-do-i-know-gods-will
**Category:** Prayer / God's will
**Question:** How do I actually figure out what God wants me to do with my life?
**Answer:** It helps to stop picturing this as one hidden master plan you have to decode. The specific decisions (career, relationships, where to live) are usually less "guess the secret code" and more "wisely choose within real freedom," using the tools God's actually given you: Scripture, prayer, wise counsel from people who know you, and honest attention to how you're wired — your gifts, your genuine desires, your circumstances. ### what-happens-when-we-die
**Category:** Life after death / identity
**Question:** What actually happens to us when we die?
**Answer:** The shared core across Christian traditions is this: physical death isn't the end of the story. Scripture consistently points toward a real future beyond it — ultimately, full bodily resurrection into a renewed creation, not a wispy, disembodied existence forever. Traditions differ on some of the in-between details — whether a person is immediately in God's presence right at death, or in a kind of rest or waiting period before the final resurrection — and that's genuinely worth a conversation with a pastor if it's something you're wrestling with. But the anchor point every tradition shares: death does not get the final word.
**Scripture:** 1 Corinthians 15:55 — "Where, O death, is your victory? Where, O death, is your sting?"
**Explore further:** My Walk — Station 13: Becoming Like Christ; Ask the Bible
**Pastor review needed:** yes — see pastor decision list #11.

**Answer:** [SENSITIVE — REVIEW] This question deserves a careful, unhurried answer, and it sits close to real pain for a lot of people, especially around identity, body, and things that feel unchosen.
