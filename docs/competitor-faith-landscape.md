# Competitor Landscape — Faith & Life Skills
_Last updated: 2026-05-06_

---

## Executive Summary

The faith-app market in 2026 is bifurcated between two large poles: YouVersion's total-free, donation-sustained Bible utility (1 billion installs, 25,000+ plans) and Hallow's premium-subscription audio-prayer experience ($69.99/year, 10,000+ sessions). Everything else occupies the middle — freemium apps gating 60–85% of their content behind subscriptions of $40–$100/year. The table-stakes layer is thick: daily devotionals, streaks, reading plans, audio narration, and prayer journaling appear in six or more competitors. Competition at that layer is already won by incumbents with multi-year head starts.

The underserved territory is precisely where YourLife CC is building: genuine all-ages family scope (not a kids app OR an adult app, but a single platform that routes by age tier), Life Skills integration with faith formation, and a donation-supported model that keeps everything behind zero Stripe gate. No current competitor in this research set does more than one of those three things. Glorify gestures at family (Bible Stories for Kids is bundled in), and Lectio 365 matches the donation model, but neither touches Life Skills or true multi-tier family routing.

The visual bar for "top 3 in market" is set by Hallow (deep dark background, premium typography, cinematic audio production) and Glorify (clean cream/off-white cards, gentle motion, editorial photography). Both are well above what most church curriculum tools offer. The indigo-plus-teal palette YLCC has locked in is competitive against both — it reads premium without being cold, and it is distinct from every dark-navy default in this category.

Content volume at launch is the most significant calibration question. Hallow shipped into a market already accustomed to Calm's deep library; it launched with several hundred sessions and has grown to 10,000+. YouVersion launched with plans but no subscription pressure, which made thin-at-launch survivable. For YLCC's Phase 6, the 24 CLG entries, 3 group arcs, 3 youth units (12 sessions), and 12 family modules are credible at launch for the donation-supported free tier — the category expectation for a free platform is lower than for a $70/year app. The volume question is less "is this enough at launch?" and more "what is the 12-month content roadmap that proves YLCC is alive and growing?"

## Where the Market is Thin

These gaps have little to no coverage across the research set. Each represents a usable opportunity zone.

**1. Genuine all-ages family routing in a single platform.** No competitor maintains a single app that routes Kids (6–11), Youth (12–17), Adults, and Family/Group leaders into age-appropriate content paths that also share data on a family account. YouVersion's kids app is a separate app. Glorify bundles Kids Stories but does not offer a youth tier or parent-child account linkage. RightNow Media is library-access only with no age-tier UX. This gap is structural — it requires architectural decisions (sub-profiles, age-tier routing, parent oversight) that these apps never designed for.

**2. Life Skills integration with faith formation.** No competitor crosses devotional content with practical life competency — budgeting, stress management, career, communication, time management. Hallow, Glorify, and Pray.com are squarely inside the prayer/devotion category. RightNow Media's library includes life-application content but it is unsorted and not mapped to any life-skill taxonomy. The "Through a Faith Lens" companion framing is original territory.

**3. Free-with-donation, no Stripe gate, no content wall.** Lectio 365 and YouVersion demonstrate the donation-funded free model, but neither offers the structured curriculum depth (group studies, youth curriculum, family modules) that makes the free content defensible against paid competitors. Bible Project is the closest analog for both free access and structural depth, but its content is animation/video-first and does not cover practical life skills or family-tier routing.

**4. Youth (12–17) as a named, designed-for tier.** Most apps treat youth as a filtered subset of adult content. Gospel Project and Foundations are curriculum-first (printable/quarterly purchase), not app-native experiences. No app-native platform has built an age-aware youth group module with session-by-session facilitation tools for a volunteer leader.

**5. Group studies with in-app session facilitation.** RightNow Media provides video access for small groups but does not provide an in-app facilitation flow (the group watches video and then uses a separate PDF). Foundations and Gospel Project are print-first. No competitor ships an app-native 4–6 session arc with built-in discussion flow, leader notes, and per-session progress tracking.

**6. Parent-managed child sub-profiles in a faith context.** Bible App for Kids is standalone. RightNow Media supports account sharing but not child-specific sub-profiles. YLCC's existing parent hub + multi-profile architecture is more sophisticated than anything in this faith-app set.

## Where YourLife CC Can Win

Five concrete positioning bets, calibrated to YLCC's locked decisions and existing architecture:

**Bet 1 — The only faith platform that routes by age, not by app.**
YLCC's existing multi-profile + age-tier architecture means a single family account can hold a parent (adult surface), a 14-year-old (youth surface), and an 8-year-old (kids surface) with a parent oversight layer. No competitor does this in one product. The pitch is not "another devotional app" — it is "the platform your family uses together, each in the right lane." This is a defensible architectural position, not a feature that can be added quickly by a YouVersion or Hallow.

**Bet 2 — Life Skills x Faith integration as a category of its own.**
"Through a Faith Lens" companion entries turn what is otherwise a two-app problem (faith app + life skills app) into one coherent surface. The faith-only user gets a taste of Life Skills with a theological frame; the paid full-app user gets the full skill plus the faith lens. This is a content strategy and a monetization bridge simultaneously. No competitor approaches this.

**Bet 3 — Free with no paywall pressure, donation optional.**
The three genuinely free competitors (YouVersion, Bible Project, Lectio 365) run a single ask model: donate to keep it free. YLCC's $5/$10/$25/$50 + custom + recurring model, embedded at non-coercive touchpoints (Day 7 streak, settings footer, donation matching modal), matches what works in this category without the 501(c)(3) halo. The key execution point is that the donation ask must never appear before the user has experienced value — this is precisely how YouVersion and Lectio 365 operate. Note: Kingdom Creatives LLC is not a 501(c)(3); donation language must be factually non-deductible per the locked T&C language in `docs/faith-only-spec.md`.

**Bet 4 — Youth Group in a box for the volunteer leader.**
Youth pastors and volunteers at mid-size and smaller churches are underserved. Gospel Project requires a quarterly print purchase. RightNow Media requires a church license and a video library model that the volunteer has to navigate. YLCC can ship 3 youth units (12 sessions) as a fully self-contained facilitation tool: hook, scripture, real talk, take-it-with-you, close, leader sidebar. At zero cost and no login friction for the students, this is adoptable in contexts where $600/year church subscriptions are a barrier.

**Bet 5 — Family Faith as a shareable parent tool, not a curriculum commitment.**
The 12-module Family Faith library — designed for 10–15 minutes at the dinner table — solves a real parent guilt problem. "I want to disciple my kids but I'm not qualified and I don't have time for a 13-week course." The format is a single sit-down, not a series. Glorify and Abide do not offer parent-to-child facilitation formats at all. This positions YLCC alongside the "Discipling Your Kids Without Performing" CLG entry (#23) in a coherent value story.

## Premium Visual Bar — Named References

What "top 3 in market" actually looks like, with specific callouts relevant to the locked indigo `#4338ca` / teal `#14b8a6` palette.

**Reference 1 — Hallow: deep dark backgrounds, cinematic audio first.**
Hallow uses a dark, near-black deep indigo/navy background (close to `#12102A`) with warm gold and cream text. Typography is large-scale serif for headings, clean sans for body. Motion is slow and deliberate — prayer sessions fade in rather than snap. The audio production quality is high-budget (Fr. Mike Schmitz, Jonathan Roumie, Bishop Barron). The takeaway for YLCC: the premium faith register is dark + serif heading + slow fade, not light + sans + sharp transition. YLCC's indigo `#4338ca` is adjacent to Hallow's palette but lighter and more vibrant — this can read as "warmer and more accessible" rather than trying to match Hallow's cinematic darkness.
(source: hallow.com, App Store screenshots)

**Reference 2 — Glorify: cream cards, editorial photography, gentle motion.**
Glorify uses a light background (off-white / cream `~#FAF9F6`) for its main surfaces with card-based layout, editorial photography (landscapes, golden-hour light), and a clean sans-serif typeface. Daily devotional cards use strong hero images. Motion is gentle — slide-in cards, not snaps. The "Pay it Forward" model and global partnership marketing (The Chosen, Tim Timberlake) make it feel like a premium publication, not a church app. The takeaway for YLCC: in light mode, cream + editorial photography + gentle card transitions outperforms corporate blue-white-grey. The teal `#14b8a6` accent pair reads well against both cream and deep dark.
(source: glorify-app.com, faith.tools, Christianity Today 2025)

**Reference 3 — Calm: minimal layout, immersive nature photography, no clutter.**
Calm's card-based layout is the gold standard for "app that never feels busy." Each session is a card with one large image, two lines of text, a play button. No chrome, no badges competing for attention. The takeaway for YLCC's faith hub: the notification bell, streak, and other engagement chrome should be contained in the shell (header, drawer) and kept off the content surfaces themselves — content cards should be uncluttered. YLCC's existing card patterns in the Faith hub are structurally close to this; the upgrade is in photography and typography scale.
(source: goodux.appcues.com/blog/calm-app-new-user-experience)

**Calibration note on the locked palette:** Indigo `#4338ca` as primary with teal `#14b8a6` secondary sits between Hallow's dark immersive register and Glorify's warm-light register. It is not trying to be either; it is a distinct brand position. The contrast with a dark shell (near-black body) or with the cream light-mode surface both work. The Phase 3 light-mode contrast pass flagged in `faith-only-spec.md` §11 remains necessary before light-mode surfaces are production-ready with these accents.

### Launch and mature targets for YLCC Phase 6

**CLG entries:** 24 total at launch (12 existing + 12 new) is comparable to a modest Abide-style library at early stage. Bible Project launched with dozens of videos but no CLG analog. Lectio 365 launched with a single daily entry and built trust through consistency. The risk is not thin volume; it is perceived depth. 24 entries with 600–1,200 word bodies is credible. A 12-month target of 36–40 entries (adding one per month) keeps the library visibly growing.

**Group Bible Studies:** 3 arcs (15 sessions total across 4-, 5-, and 6-session arcs) at launch is tight but defensible. RightNow Media's video series run 4–12 sessions; Gospel Project runs 13 per quarter. The YLCC 4–6 session format is structurally correct for the market. 6–9 arcs (three waves of 3) by end of year one puts YLCC inside a credible library range.

**Youth curriculum:** 3 units / 12 sessions at launch matches what a small ministry platform would ship first. Gospel Project ships 13 per quarter per age group, but that is print-primary with a 30-year brand behind it. For an app-native youth facilitation tool, 12 sessions is a viable first cohort. 6 units / 24 sessions by end of year one is a reasonable target.

**Family Faith modules:** 12 at launch is the right call. These are short, self-contained modules (10–15 min), not ongoing series. 12 is enough to cover a full quarter of weekly family meals. 24 by end of year one keeps the feature alive.

**Calibration answer A (from the assignment brief):** Leading apps do not have a direct CLG analog to benchmark against. Hallow's "hundreds of free sessions" at launch scaled to 10,000+ over 4+ years. Bible Project launched with 10–15 videos and now has 300+. For a donation-funded free platform, 24 CLG entries is above the threshold for credibility at launch. The question is update cadence, not launch volume.

### How the donation-funded free model works in practice

**YouVersion:** All content free, no ads, no subscription. Donations solicited via a dedicated giving page (give.bible.com) and an in-app link. The ask language centers on "keep it free for everyone." Monthly recurring ("Sowers Community") is emphasized alongside one-time giving. YouVersion is affiliated with Life.Church, a 501(c)(3) — donations are tax-deductible (source: youversion.com/giving). This is a key difference from YLCC. The YouVersion model is the purest "everything free, donate if moved" approach in the market.

**Bible Project:** 100% free, 501(c)(3) nonprofit, crowdfunded from launch. The donation ask in the app is a non-intrusive button (not a paywall, not a modal interrupt). Monthly support is the primary pitch; patron gifts ($50+/month) receive physical thank-you items. Tax-deductible. Bible Project's model demonstrates that depth + free can coexist if the content quality earns loyalty (source: bibleproject.com/give).

**Lectio 365:** Free, sustained by donations to 24-7 Prayer. No in-app purchase. The give page emphasizes "help us keep it free and increasingly accessible." Monthly supporters and major/legacy gifts are both options. No 501(c)(3) equivalent stated in available sources — 24-7 Prayer is a UK-registered charity (source: lectio365.com/give).

**Critical flag for YLCC — 501(c)(3) assumption:** Both YouVersion and Bible Project are 501(c)(3) nonprofits. Their donation asks work in part because donors can claim a tax deduction. Lectio 365 is a UK charity; its US donors cannot deduct. Kingdom Creatives LLC is none of these. YLCC's donation ask must be explicitly non-deductible as required by the locked T&C language in `faith-only-spec.md`. The donation CTA copy must not imply or suggest deductibility. Patterns like "support this ministry" (YouVersion) are usable; patterns like "your tax-deductible gift" (common in nonprofit apps) are not.

**Where the ask appears in top performers:**
- YouVersion: Give link in main menu + dedicated give page + occasional blog post; never a modal interrupt mid-session.
- Bible Project: In-app donate button in the app drawer; give page prominently linked from website; not gated behind sessions.
- Lectio 365: Give link on website; app does not aggressively prompt.

**YLCC's three-surface ask model** (footer/settings link, Day 7 streak soft card, donation-match modal) closely follows what works. The Day 7 soft card is structurally similar to YouVersion's streak-based engagement but appropriately delayed. The donation-match modal is an opportunity YouVersion does not use; it is genuinely novel in the category.

**Recurring vs. one-time:** YouVersion emphasizes recurring (Sowers Community framing). Bible Project offers both. Lectio 365 offers both. Pray.com abandoned one-time in favor of subscriptions. The market data supports offering both on one screen — YLCC's locked decision to put one-time + recurring toggle on the same Embedded Payment Element screen is consistent with what converts in this category.

### Hallow-specific monetization note

Hallow gates approximately 85% of content, making its free tier functional but clearly limited (source: aeanet.org, help.hallow.com). Its paid tier is priced at parity with Calm and Headspace — a deliberate positioning move to normalize a faith subscription at the same value as a secular wellness subscription. The 3-month free trial available in some campaigns is an aggressive conversion mechanism. Hallow's subscription revenue model is not available for donation comparison, as it is pure subscription. The "money grab" criticism in App Store / BBB reviews is driven primarily by auto-renewal billing disputes, not content quality (source: ibtimes.co.uk, bbb.org/hallow).

### YouVersion (Bible.com)

- **Core proposition:** The world's largest free Bible platform — every translation, every reading plan, social engagement, zero cost.
- **Pricing:** 100% free, no ads, no in-app purchases, no subscription. Donation-supported via the Sowers Community (recurring) and one-time gifts at give.bible.com. Affiliated with Life.Church (501(c)(3)) — donations are tax-deductible. (source: youversion.com/giving, help.youversion.com/giving)
- **Standout features:** (1) 25,000+ reading plans in 100+ languages; (2) social plan sharing — invite friends to read a plan together with shared highlights; (3) 1 billion installs, 22 million users on New Year's Day 2025 — scale creates social proof loop. (source: youversion.com, protestia.com Nov 2025)
- **Content depth:** 3,500+ Bible versions; 25,000+ reading plans; daily verse; community events. Plans are added continuously by partner publishers — the library is effectively unlimited. No group-study facilitation tools; no life-skills content. (source: YouVersion blog)
- **Audience tiers:** Adults and families primarily; Bible App for Kids is a separate app (ages 4–8); no native youth (12–17) tier; no structured small-group arc.
- **Visual register:** Clean, functional, light-mode default. Utility-first — large Bible text, minimal chrome. Not premium; not dated. Readable and unpretentious. (source: App Store screenshots)
- **Notable UX moves:** Verse-of-the-Day widget is a daily touchpoint outside the app; Friends feature lets users see each other's reading streaks and highlights, turning a personal habit into a social signal; plan-sharing nudge at the end of each plan drives re-engagement.
- **Weakness:** No structured group study or youth facilitation; zero editorial curation (anyone can submit a plan, leading to doctrine quality concerns from users); note/highlight management is weak (chronological only, no tagging or sorting). (source: christianbytes.com, kingdomharbor.com 2024)
- **Sources:** [youversion.com](https://www.youversion.com/bible-app), [YouVersion giving](https://www.youversion.com/giving), [Protestia 1B milestone](https://protestia.com/2025/11/19/youversion-bible-app-celebrates-1-billion-downloads/), [YouVersion Support](https://help.youversion.com/)

### Hallow

- **Core proposition:** Premium Catholic-leaning audio prayer and meditation app, styled after Calm but anchored in Christian spiritual tradition.
- **Pricing:** Free tier with ~15% of library (daily prayer, basic meditation, limited readings). Premium: $69.99/yr (individual), $10.99/mo, $119.99/yr (Family plan, up to 6 users), $149.99 lifetime. Free trial 7–14 days. Pricing confirmed 2025. (source: help.hallow.com, thedolceway.com 2026)
- **Standout features:** (1) Audio-first prayer sessions narrated by recognizable Catholic voices (Fr. Mike Schmitz, Jonathan Roumie, Bishop Barron); (2) Routines — user schedules prayer times and receives push notification cues; (3) Challenges — community prayer events (Lent, Advent) that drive mass re-engagement. 750 million+ prayers completed as of February 2025. (source: hallow.com, catholicculture.org)
- **Content depth:** 10,000+ sessions including prayers, meditations, sleep content, audio Bible (Bible in a Year with Fr. Mike Schmitz), music, Rosary, Examen, novenas, Saints. Weekly new content drops; daily devotional. (source: hallow.com)
- **Audience tiers:** Adults and older teens; Bedtime Prayers for Kids bundled in premium. No youth (12–17) facilitated tier. No family account routing. No group study tools. Primarily individual spiritual practice. (source: hallow.com/features)
- **Visual register:** Dark, near-black deep indigo/navy backgrounds with warm gold and cream text. Premium serif headings. Large hero photography (candles, nature, sacred spaces). Slow fade transitions. Cinematic audio production quality sets the aesthetic expectation. (source: App Store screenshots, hallow.com)
- **Notable UX moves:** The Routines feature is the most sophisticated notification system in the faith app space — users build a prayer queue that fires at scheduled times; individual items in the queue can be locked (same session daily) or rotating (next in a collection). This is not a simple "remind me to pray" push — it is a full prayer scheduler. (source: help.hallow.com/routines)
- **Weakness:** ~85% paywall means new users hit friction quickly; billing complaints are the top App Store and BBB complaint category (auto-renewal, poor cancellation UX). Heavy Catholic weighting alienates Protestant and evangelical users. No group facilitation or family account structure. EU access issues emerged in 2024 (restricted in some markets). (source: ibtimes.co.uk 2024, bbb.org/hallow, aeanet.org)

## Calibration Answers (Inline)

**A. How many devotional / CLG-style entries do leading apps ship at launch vs. mature?**

No competitor has a direct CLG analog (a curated long-form guide library indexed by life topic, not a daily devotional feed). The closest proxies: Hallow launched with "several hundred" sessions and scaled to 10,000+ over ~4 years. Bible Project launched with approximately 10–15 videos and now has 300+. Abide's 2,000+ meditations represent a library built over years. For a donation-supported free platform, 24 CLG entries at launch is above the credibility threshold — it is the update cadence that matters. One new entry per month (reaching 36 by end of year one) is the minimum signal of an actively maintained platform. YLCC's 24-entry launch is not thin; the risk is user perception that the library has not grown 6 months in.
(source: hallow.com, bibleproject.com/app, abide.com)

**B. What's the typical session length and arc structure for group Bible studies in RightNow Media and Gospel Project?**

RightNow Media: video segments run 8–22 minutes per session; most series run 4–12 sessions with a downloadable PDF study guide. There is no in-app facilitation flow — the video + PDF combination defines the format. Groups can be 4–12 people; the platform tracks video completion but not discussion quality. (source: rightnowmedia.org/blog/youth-groups, support.rightnowmedia.org)

Gospel Project (Students): 13 sessions per quarter, run weekly, implying approximately 45–60 minute sessions in a Sunday School or midweek context. The Leader Guide structures each session with a teaching point, discussion questions, and application. The quarterly cadence is predictable and renewable. (source: lifeway.com/gospel-project/students)

YLCC calibration: The 4–6 session arc format is below Gospel Project's 13-per-quarter but above RightNow Media's shortest series. A 4-session arc is the right minimum commitment for discovery; a 6-session arc tests whether a group will stay for a full month-and-a-half. The 30–45 minute session format falls between RightNow's 8–22 min video-only model and Gospel Project's 45–60 min full-class model. This is appropriate for a mid-week small group context. The format choice is defensible.

**C. How do Hallow, Pray.com, and Bible Project handle the donation/freemium boundary?**

Hallow: Hard paywall — ~85% of content gated behind a $69.99/yr subscription. There is no donation option; the subscription IS the revenue model. The free tier has enough content to be useful but not enough to be satisfying long-term. The free trial (7–14 days) is the conversion mechanism. (source: help.hallow.com, aeanet.org)

Pray.com: Hard paywall at ~$99.99/yr. Evolved away from donations entirely. Free tier has daily prayers with ads; premium removes ads and unlocks celebrity series and new programs. The Giving feature (routing user donations to churches) is separate from app access and has unclear fee structure. (source: businessmodelcanvas, help.pray.com)

Bible Project: No paywall. Everything free. Donation link in app drawer and on website — non-intrusive, not session-gated. Monthly patron support framing ("your gift makes all of this possible") is consistent with their educational-nonprofit identity. In-app giving accepts one-time and monthly; no embedded payment element inside the app itself — links to bibleproject.com/give. 501(c)(3) status means US donors can deduct. (source: bibleproject.com/give, help.bibleproject.com)

YLCC calibration: The "no paywall, embedded donation" model is most analogous to Bible Project, not to Hallow or Pray.com. The key difference is that Bible Project's donation ask is genuinely non-intrusive (menu link) while YLCC's three-surface model (footer, Day 7 streak, donation-match modal) is more structured. The Day 7 timing and the matching-campaign highlight are both absent from Bible Project's approach and represent genuine improvements on the model. The 501(c)(3) gap is real and should be addressed in CTA copy: "donations support Kingdom Creatives LLC and are not tax-deductible" must be visible adjacent to every donation prompt.

**D. What does Hallow's notification system actually do, and how often does it engage users?**

Hallow's notification system is built around the Routines feature: users schedule prayer sessions at specific times of day (e.g., 7:00 AM, 12:30 PM, 9:00 PM) and receive push notifications when each time block arrives. Within a routine, individual prayer sessions can be set to repeat the same session daily or rotate through a collection. The bell icon in the app shows a notification badge for unread items. Users can manage notification preferences per session type in the Me tab. (source: help.hallow.com/routines, help.hallow.com/notifications)

The notification system is deliberately tied to user-set schedules, not algorithm-driven broadcast. This is a retention-positive pattern: users who set their own prayer times are pre-committed to the prompt, reducing opt-out. Industry data (not Hallow-specific) shows: one push/week = 440% higher retention than zero; daily push = 820% higher retention; opt-out risk increases above 5–6 per week; 10% of users disable notifications after one weekly push, rising to 40% after 3–6 weekly pushes. Best practice is 1–3 targeted/behavioral pushes per week. (source: mobiloud.com/push-notification-statistics, businessofapps.com/push-notifications)
